The making of 'The New Look', the TV series about Christian Dior

August 28, 2024
blog

On the opening night of London Fashion Week, in association with the British Fashion Council, Bazaar hosted a very special preview screening of Apple TV+'s extraordinary miniseries, The New Look (streaming now). Charting the rise of one of fashion's greatest luminaries, Christian Dior, the show is a dramatic take on how the designer, his family and his fashion contemporaries navigated occupied Paris during the Second World War, and the role their creativity paid in bringing the city back to life in its aftermath.

Prior to the screening, Bazaar's features editor, Charlotte Brook, took to the stage with Bazaar's former editor-in-chief, Justine Picardie (and author of Miss Dior), and The New Look's costume designer, Karen Muller Serreau. Their conversation covered the vital historical context of the show, the inspiring – and still relatively unknown – story of Christian's sister, Catherine, and the challenges of bringing Dior's earliest creations to the screen.

(From left) Charlotte Brook, Justine Picardie and Karen Muller Serreau hosted a screening to discuss the new series

"I hope this series will bring the story of this immensely courageous young woman to a larger audience and to a new generation," said Picardie, of the new spotlight now being shone on the inspiring and heart-breaking story of wartime hero Catherine Dior, played by Bazaar's current cover star, Maisie Williams.

Picardie spoke of the intense bond the Dior siblings shared, forged by poverty and tragedy, and how integral this relationship was to the great designer's creations. His inaugural collection was dedicated to a "flower woman": that woman was Catherine Dior, who at the time had become a horticulturist, producing the roses which would form the base of the house's very first perfume, Miss Dior, named in her honour.

Roger Do Minh//Apple

Maisie Williams as Catherine Dior in The New Look

"Catherine was the woman he loved best in the world, and she was his first model," Picardie explained. "She was emaciated when she returned to France, from the concentration camps, so the soft padding you'll find in the Bar jacket, on the hips, the bust and the shoulders, provide a kind of protection to his beloved younger sister, who he had not been able to protect. I find that tenderness really emotional."

The fashion of The New Look is, of course, a foundational tenet of the show and a character in its own right. For esteemed costume designer Muller Serreau, capturing such an iconic moment in fashion was a unique challenge, alongside costuming the wartime years and sartorial changes in the cast. For Williams (whom she praised for "doing all her own stunts") the task was to dress "an everyday person who could hide and blend in", who then becomes one of the very first Dior models.

Roger Do Minh//Apple

Christian Dior himself is played by Ben Mendelsohn in the series

"We were incredibly careful with the costumes that we created for the fashion shows," she said. "We put cloth down on the ground, the girls that were dressing [the models] had to wear gloves and we had to shout to everybody on the set to move everything out of the way to make the space for them to get through."

The costumes were made with incredible attention to detail, the corsetry and padding all made to replicate some of fashion's most formational looks. Extreme wartime rationing had produced such malnourishment that Muller Serreau noted how shocked she was by the miniscule measurements of the original garments. Picardie shared a meeting she had with the late Pierre Cardin, who led the first Dior atelier and told her he still remembered the extraordinary proportions – "those tiny, tiny waists" – of 1947's 'The New Look.'

"I hope this series will bring the story of this immensely courageous young woman to a larger audience and to a new generation," said Picardie of Catherine Dior

For Muller Serreau, the show is a love letter to the resilience of her own craft. "In occupied France, the Nazis wanted to take the fashion industry to Berlin," she said. "And so living and working in this industry in Paris today, it means so much. This story is about survival and how creative people use their imagination to get through difficult moments."

Picardie added that she hopes people will take the message of optimism and rebirth from the story, before sharing how much Bazaar itself is enmeshed in the history of Dior.

"It was Carmel Snow, Bazaar's editor at the time, who coined the phrase," she said. "That first collection was called Corolle, but rather than saying that, which probably wouldn't have translated very well for the readers of Harper's Bazaar, she says, 'Ah, my dear Christian, it's such a new look!'"

'The New Look' is streaming now on Apple TV+
