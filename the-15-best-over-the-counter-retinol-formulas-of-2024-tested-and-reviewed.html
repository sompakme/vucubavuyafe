If you were to ask any skincare expert what ingredient is non-negotiable, their first would be SPF, with retinol being a close second. Considered the gold standard in anti-aging and acne treatment, it promotes firmer skin, smoothes out fine lines and wrinkles, minimizes pores, evens out skin tone, and clears up blemishes. And from our experience, it really is that powerful. 

Despite being a powerhouse, retinol can get a bit of a bad rap due to its potential for causing irritation — but that’s not to say you shouldn’t give it a go. What it all comes down to is choosing the right formula for your needs. There are hundreds of options available to you, ranging from serums and moisturizers to boosters and targeted treatments, all at a variety of price points, so there’s something out there for everyone. The only challenge is finding your perfect match. 

Instead of spending hours of time doing research, rely on ours. We meticulously tested 32 of the best over-the-counter retinol formulas on the market for 12 weeks, rating each one for their absorption and consistency, as well as the effect it had on our skin, and final results it provided. We then cut down that list to the top-performing products.

What to Keep in Mind

Your Skin Type

The majority of people can add retinol into their skincare regimen, including those with deeper skin tones, as there’s no risk of causing discoloration. In fact, retinol helps even out skin tone. 

“Most skin types would benefit from using a retinol serum, particularly sun damaged mature skin types, acne or oily skin, and those with hyperpigmentation and or melasma,” confirms New York-based board certified plastic surgeon Dr. Lyle Leipziger.

However, some people just can’t tolerate the potency of retinoids, even over-the-counter retinol. “Some can experience redness, flakiness, and irritation,” says Colorado-based board certified dermatologist Dr. Kenneth Mark. This goes for people of any skin type (which is why patch testing is so important), but it’s especially true of those with very sensitive skin types. 

While this has nothing to do with your skin type, you should not use retinol if you are pregnant or breastfeeding. Instead, you can opt for formulas that contain plant-based retinol alternatives like bakuchiol, which you’ll find in BYBI Beauty 1% Bakuchiol + Olive Squalane Oil Booster and Biossance Squalane + Phyto-Retinol Serum.

Retinol Concentration

Retinol products come in a wide variety of concentrations, which simply refers to the potency of the ingredient in the formulation. “0.25 percent is the lowest strength both in the prescription form and in over-the-counter versions,” says Dr. Leipziger. “Dry, fragile, or thin skins should start with 0.25 percent on alternate nights and gradually build up to at least five nights per week to experience the best anti-aging benefits.” You can find an even gentler 0.2 percent in Common Heir Retinol Serum. 

If you’re already a retinol user, have thicker skin, or are not particularly sensitive, you can try a middle of the road formula, which will have a 0.3 or 0.5 percent concentration; the highest possible concentration is one percent. “I would reserve the 1% for thicker, oily skins,” says Dr. Leipziger. “It can be appropriate for acne sufferers, but slow and steady often results in the best outcome.” 

When you first start using retinol, you might experience what is known as “the retinol uglies,” although skin reactions will vary from person to person. “For some people, their skin may just be red, dry, flaky, and irritated for a few days as their skin adjusts to it,” says Dr. Mark. In other cases, it may cause a full-on skin purge, pushing congestion out of the pores that results in breakouts; this could last for anywhere from one week to two months, which is the main reason you want to ease into retinoid use. Starting a retinol regimen can be a shock to the system, so don’t be afraid to take it slow with a lower concentration. 

You might find that some brands do not disclose the concentration of retinol in their formulas. This is not necessarily a bad thing, as it may mean that it’s blended with other potent ingredients to deliver the best results without being irritating. It could also be a sign that the formula actually features the aforementioned bakuchiol, a plant-based retinol alternative, which is non-sensitizing yet provides comparable results. 

Encapsulated Formulas

You may see the retinol in your product being referred to as “time-release” or “encapsulated.” That means the formula’s active ingredient is literally wrapped in a microscopic shell that slowly melts after it's applied. “Encapsulation ensures the retinol is gradually released into the skin, allowing it to be more tolerable, as well as bathe the skin in a 24-hour cycle of the ingredient, which can be more beneficial,” says Dr. Leipziger’s medical esthetician, Kerry Elmasry. If your complexion tends to be a bit more sensitive, but you’d like to incorporate this potent ingredient into your routine, an encapsulated retinol product like Shani Darden Skin Care Retinol Reform Treatment Serum may be the way to go.

Hydrating + Nourishing Ingredients 

Because retinol can be notoriously drying on the skin, you’ll want to bring in reinforcements — namely ingredients that will boost hydration levels, protect your moisture barrier, and seal in nourishment. “I really like combining retinoids with hyaluronic acid for increased moisturization,” says Dr. Mark. You’ll find retinol paired with hyaluronic acid in LilyAna Naturals Retinol Cream for Face. 

Ceramides are another good pick: These fatty acids are naturally found in the skin and act as the mortar between the “bricks” of your skin cells, preventing moisture from escaping and allowing skin to stay balanced and comfortable. They make up the foundation of CeraVe’s Resurfacing Retinol Serum.

Another great ingredient to combine with retinol is niacinamide. Not only can niacinamide amp up hydration, but it can help lighten discoloration. Dr. Mark adds that it can also increase tolerability, calm the skin, and provide additional anti-aging benefits, as we saw in the Youth To The People Retinal + Niacinamide Youth Serum.

Your Questions, Answered

What does retinol do for skin?

Retinol is a true icon in the world of skincare, and that’s because it does pretty much everything. “Retinol is anti-aging, decreases hyperpigmentation, increases collagen and elastin, improves fine lines and wrinkles, smoothes, exfoliates, delivers a more radiant complexion, and decreases rough spots, pre-cancers, and skin cancers,” says Dr. Mark. When paired with other active ingredients like vitamin C, retinol can also be very effective in dark spot correcting formulas.

The prescription-strength version of retinol, retinoic acid, is the only ingredient that’s been clinically proven to reduce the signs of aging.

Is OTC retinol effective?

Yes, though our experts will tell you it’s not as good as the prescription-strength version. “It isn't as active, powerful, or studied as thoroughly,” says Dr. Mark. But while retinol is weaker than retinoic acid, you’ll still get all the same benefits from using the former. It'll just take longer: One study shows that a prescription-strength retinoid takes about a month to significantly improve the look of fine lines and wrinkles, but an over-the-counter retinol provides the same results in about 12 weeks. 

If you’re willing to wait, it’s worth the reward — and because retinol isn’t as potent as retinoic acid, you won’t experience as much irritation, which we’d call a win.

What's the strongest OTC retinol?

The strongest retinol you’ll find over the counter is adapalene, which is the main ingredient in the Differin Gel Acne Treatment. “Differin was originally formulated for acne patients, but if your skin is thick and or oily, you can achieve the same benefits,” says Elmasry. This product contains one percent of the active ingredient.

What strength retinol is best for aging?

The higher the dose of retinol your skin can tolerate, the better, but don’t jump in with both feet. “Mature skin tends to be drier and can break out in a rash if you initially start people on a higher dose,” warns Elmasry. 

Instead, start with a gentle formula once a week as your skin adjusts to it, building your way towards an application cadence of several times weekly. If you’ve finished the bottle and your skin has fully tolerated the product, you’re ready to graduate to a formula with a higher concentration.

Do you put moisturizer or retinol on first?

You may have heard of the “retinol sandwich” technique, which consists of applying a thin layer of moisturizer both before and after putting on a retinol product, but our experts don’t recommend this. “Retinol should always be applied as the first application to clean, dry skin,” says Dr. Leipziger. 


The whole point of using a skincare product is to reap all of its benefits — applying a moisturizer before your retinol will dilute its effects. If you’re looking for less reactivity, use a weaker formula instead.
